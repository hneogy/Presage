The honesty mechanic

When a prediction resolves, Présage hides your original confidence and asks you Yes / No / Ambiguous first. Only after you answer does it reveal what you said. This deliberate friction prevents the most common form of self-deception: anchoring on your past confidence and finding ways to call yourself "basically right."

Step 1 · Predict

"I'll finish the book by Friday."

You commit to a claim, a confidence percentage, and a resolution date. Confidence ranges 50–99% — anything below 50% is the same forecast inverted, so flip the claim instead.

Step 2 · Resolve

"Did it happen?" 73%

Your confidence is hidden. You answer Yes, No, or Ambiguous based only on what actually happened — not on what you wanted to have happened.

Step 3 · Reveal

"You said 73% — and you were right."

Now you see the score. Brier score updates, calibration curve shifts, your category and horizon breakdowns all recalculate. Over time, the gap between confidence and reality narrows.

The math

Brier score is a proper scoring rule. You can't game it. Saying 70% when you really think 90% will hurt your score — that's what makes calibration measurable instead of theatre.

Brier = (forecast − outcome)²
where forecast is your stated confidence (0.5–0.99) and outcome is 1 if it happened, 0 if it didn't.
0.00Perfect
0.25Random (always 50%)
1.00Maximally wrong
